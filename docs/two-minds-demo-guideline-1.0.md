# The Two Minds Demo

## Part I — The Vision

### The room

Picture a living room. A laptop connected to a television, or a tablet propped on the dinner table. Your family is seated — some curious, some skeptical, one probably already looking at their phone. You have maybe ninety seconds before their attention locks in or drifts away.

On the screen: two faces. Not photorealistic — illustrated, stylized, warm. On the left, a woman named **Luna**. Short description beneath her portrait: *"Thoughtful. Cautious. Feels things deeply."* On the right, a man named **Marco**. His description: *"Curious. Bold. Bounces back fast."*

Between them, a simple card with a scenario written on it. Below each portrait, three small horizontal bars — labeled plainly as **Mood**, **Energy**, and **Calm** — all starting near the middle. And a small rounded tag, currently empty, where an emotion label will appear.

You tap a button. The scenario card reads:

> *"You've just been offered a big promotion at work. The catch: you have to move to a city where you don't know a single person."*

A beat of silence. Then Luna begins to speak.

---

### Luna speaks first

Her voice is warm but measured. A slight hesitation before she starts, as if she's been turning the thought over. The ElevenLabs voice you've chosen for her has a lower register, a deliberate pacing, a tendency to trail off slightly at the end of sentences as if she's not quite done thinking.

*"That's... a lot. I mean, the promotion is wonderful — I've been working toward something like this for years. But leaving everything? My friends, my neighborhood, the coffee shop where they already know my order? I keep imagining myself in some empty apartment, in a city where nobody knows my name."*

As she speaks, her Mood bar dips gently from center toward the left. Her Energy bar stays roughly where it is — she's not depleted, she's *conflicted.* The emotion tag fades in: **Apprehension.** Your family can see that this isn't just a scripted sad response. Something underneath is moving.

She continues, a touch quieter:

*"I know I'd regret not taking it. That's almost worse. I'd spend the rest of the year wondering what if. But right now, all I can feel is this tightness in my chest."*

Her Mood is now visibly left of center. Calm has dipped slightly. The emotion tag shifts: **Ambivalence.** Not anxiety, not sadness — something more specific, more human. Your family sees it happen in real time.

---

### Marco responds

His voice is different. Brighter, faster, with a rhythmic confidence. The ElevenLabs voice has more air in it, slightly higher pitch, a cadence that leans forward into sentences rather than trailing off.

*"Are you kidding? I'd take that in a heartbeat. New city, new people, a whole chapter I haven't written yet — that's exactly the kind of thing I want more of. Yeah, I'll miss people, but they're not going anywhere. We've got phones. We've got planes."*

His Mood bar ticks upward. Energy rises slightly. The emotion tag reads: **Excitement.**

He goes on, with a laugh in his voice:

*"Honestly, the scariest part for me would be if I stayed and had to watch someone else take it. That would eat me alive. No — this one's easy. When do I start?"*

Mood is clearly positive. Energy is high. The emotion tag holds on **Excitement.** Your family is watching two people have completely different emotional reactions to the same news. Not different opinions — different *experiences.*

Someone at the table says: "Luna's me."

That's the moment the demo has worked.

---

### The second blow

You don't wait. You tap the next scenario. The card updates:

> *"Your best friend calls, upset. They say your decision to move feels like you're abandoning them."*

Now the emotional residue matters.

Luna, who was already carrying apprehension, drops. Her Mood bar slides further left. The Calm bar descends. You can see, visually, that she was already fragile before this hit. Her voice, when it comes, is thinner:

*"That's exactly what I was afraid of. I haven't even decided yet and I'm already hurting someone. Maybe they're right. Maybe I am being selfish."*

The emotion tag shifts to **Guilt.** Not because a heuristic fired — because in the context of what Luna has already been through (apprehension, ambivalence, the weight of leaving), this new blow lands as guilt. The same prediction error pattern — negative social feedback — would feel different to a different person, in a different emotional state, coming from a different history. The system knows this. Your family doesn't need to know how. They just see it.

Marco, riding high from excitement, absorbs the same call differently. His Mood dips, but only slightly. The emotion tag reads: **Discomfort** — not guilt, not defensiveness. Something lighter.

*"Oof. That's hard to hear. I love them and I don't want them to feel that way. But I also know — and they know, deep down — that I'd be miserable if I stayed out of obligation. I think they're scared, not angry. We'll work through it."*

His Mood has recovered already. His Energy is still high. The tag fades back to neutral. He's processed it and moved on. Luna hasn't.

Your family can see both agents' histories — the bars, the emotion labels, the trajectory. One is spiraling inward. The other has absorbed a hit and kept moving. They have the same information. They heard the same phone call. The difference is who they *are.*

---

### Three months later

You tap the third scenario:

> *"It's been three months in the new city. You still haven't made close friends. Saturday nights are quiet."*

This is where the temporal depth becomes undeniable.

Luna is entering this scenario already depleted. Her Mood has been negative for two rounds. Her Calm is low. Her Energy has been draining under sustained negative affect. When she speaks, the voice has changed — not because you scripted it, but because ElevenLabs is receiving state-modulated parameters. Slower. Quieter. Flatter.

*"I knew this would happen. I keep telling myself it'll get better, but every Saturday I just sit there, and the silence is so loud. I've started wondering if I even like who I'm becoming here — someone who eats dinner alone and pretends it's fine."*

The emotion tag reads: **Loneliness** — but the self-model telemetry, if you were watching it (you're not showing this to your family), would also show elevated identity drift. She's questioning who she is. Not just unhappy — *disoriented.*

Marco, entering from a position of mild discomfort but high energy and resilient mood:

*"Yeah, the Saturday thing is real. I won't lie, there have been a couple of nights where I've thought 'what am I doing here.' But then Sunday comes and I find a new neighborhood to explore, or I sign up for something random — last week it was a ceramics class, which was terrible, and I loved it. The friends will come. I'm not worried."*

His Mood is slightly below center — he's honest about the difficulty — but his Energy is still high and the emotion tag reads: **Resolve.** Not denial. Not forced optimism. Resolve, because his personality — low neuroticism, high openness, high resilience — constructs that emotional meaning from the same lonely Saturday nights.

Someone at the table — maybe the one who said "Luna's me" — says: "Can she be okay?"

You say: "That depends on what happens next. Want to give her something?"

---

### The open floor

Now the room takes over. Someone says, "Okay, what if Luna runs into an old friend on the street." You type it in, or say it aloud if you've wired speech-to-text. The scenario card updates. Luna responds — and because she's been carrying two rounds of negative affect, the encounter with the old friend doesn't just make her happy. It breaks something loose. The Mood bar jumps. The emotion tag reads **Relief** — not happiness, not excitement. Relief, because in the context of accumulated loneliness and guilt, a familiar face is a lifeline, not a pleasant surprise.

Someone else tries: "What if Marco gets fired?" Now the adventurer takes a hit. His Mood drops, his Energy drops — but watch the recovery. Within the response, you can see him reframe. The emotion tag might flash **Shock**, then settle into **Defiance** or **Determination.** His personality doesn't prevent the pain; it shapes the recovery arc.

Someone tries to stump the system: "What if they both have to babysit a difficult toddler?" The agents respond — the scenario activates different personality dimensions (patience maps to Agreeableness and Conscientiousness, frustration tolerance to Resilience), the state bars move modestly, and the emotion tags show something like **Mild Stress** for Luna and **Amusement** for Marco. Not every scenario is dramatic. That's honest. That's the point.

The key: between all of these scenarios, state carries forward. The agents don't reset. Luna's bad night colors her response to the toddler. Marco's firing, if it happened, colors his response to the next thing. Your family begins to intuit, without being told, that these agents have something like memory, something like an emotional arc, something like a self that persists.

---

### The reveal

After the open floor — maybe ten minutes in, maybe twenty, however long the energy lasts — you do one final move. You say: "Okay. Now watch."

You tap a button labeled **Swap.** The portraits stay the same. The names stay the same. But Luna now has Marco's personality traits, and Marco has Luna's.

You replay the original promotion scenario.

Luna — cautious, careful Luna — speaks with a new energy. She takes the promotion eagerly. She brushes off the friend's call. She handles the lonely Saturdays with curious exploration. The bars move differently. The emotions are different. Everything about the experience is different, even though the "person" has the same name and the same face.

Your family realizes: it was never about the script. It was never about the scenario. It was about the *personality underneath.* That realization — felt, not explained — is the demo's thesis, delivered without a single equation.

---

### What you do not say

At no point during the demonstration do you say "precision-weighted prediction error." You do not mention Friston, Boltzmann, or active inference. You do not explain what the bars represent mathematically. You do not use the words "architecture," "hierarchy," or "generative model."

If someone asks "how does it work," you say: "Each agent has a personality profile — like a set of tendencies. Some people are more sensitive to stress. Some are more curious. Some hold on to who they are more tightly. The system uses those tendencies to figure out not just what the agent would *say*, but how they would *feel*, and how that feeling would shape the next moment."

If they push further: "It's like the difference between an actor reading lines and someone actually living through something. The agent doesn't just have a script for 'sad' — it arrives at sadness through a specific path, based on who it is and what it's been through."

That's enough. The demo spoke for itself.

### Component 1: The two agents

Each agent is an instance of the existing Red Iron Square simulation stack. No new engine is required. The current `SelfAwareSimulator` already produces personality-dependent decisions, emotional state trajectories, and self-model dynamics — more than enough for a compelling demo.

**Luna's personality profile:**

```json
{
  "O": 0.3, "C": 0.7, "E": 0.3, "A": 0.8,
  "N": 0.8, "R": 0.3, "I": 0.7, "T": 0.7
}
```

Low openness (prefers the familiar), high conscientiousness (plans carefully), low extraversion (drained by novelty), high agreeableness (cares deeply about others' feelings), high neuroticism (feels stress acutely), low resilience (recovers slowly), high idealism (holds herself to high standards), high tradition (values consistency and continuity).

**Marco's personality profile:**

```json
{
  "O": 0.9, "C": 0.3, "E": 0.8, "A": 0.5,
  "N": 0.2, "R": 0.9, "I": 0.4, "T": 0.2
}
```

High openness (drawn to novelty), low conscientiousness (flexible, improvises), high extraversion (energized by interaction), moderate agreeableness (empathetic but not self-sacrificing), low neuroticism (steady under stress), high resilience (bounces back fast), low idealism (pragmatic), low tradition (embraces change).

These profiles are chosen to produce maximum visible divergence across the scripted scenario arc. They are not caricatures — they represent recognizable human types.

**For the personality swap in Act 3:** simply exchange the JSON payloads between agents. Keep names, portraits, and voice IDs the same. The audience sees the same "person" respond differently because the underlying personality changed.

### Component 2: The scenario enrichment service

When a family member types or speaks a free-form scenario ("what if Luna's cat gets sick"), the system must convert it into a structured scenario with dimension activations that the simulator can consume.

**Input:** Free-text scenario description from the audience.

**Process:** A single LLM call (via the existing `AgentRuntime`) with a prompt like:

```
You convert natural-language situations into personality-simulation scenarios.
Given a situation description, return a JSON object with:
- "name": short identifier
- "description": one-sentence summary
- "values": object mapping dimension keys (O, C, E, A, N, R, I, T) to
  activation levels between 0.0 and 1.0, representing how much this
  situation demands or activates each personality dimension.

Example: "You get into a heated argument with a coworker" →
{"name": "workplace_conflict", "description": "...",
 "values": {"O": 0.2, "C": 0.4, "E": 0.7, "A": 0.8, "N": 0.7,
            "R": 0.5, "I": 0.3, "T": 0.3}}
```

**Output:** A validated `Scenario` object, passed to both agents' `tick()` methods.

**Latency budget:** This call takes ~1–2 seconds. It happens once per audience scenario, not per agent. The enrichment can begin immediately on input; by the time the frontend finishes displaying the scenario card, the structured scenario is ready.

**Fallback:** If the LLM returns an invalid payload, use a neutral default scenario (all dimensions at 0.5) and let the agents respond based on accumulated state alone. Graceful degradation — the response may be blander, but the demo doesn't break.

### Component 3: The response generation pipeline

For each scenario, each agent goes through this sequence:

**Step 1 — Simulation tick.** The `SelfAwareSimulator.tick()` method consumes the enriched scenario and (optionally) a forced outcome or lets the stochastic outcome model resolve. This produces: chosen action, action probabilities, state before/after (mood, energy, arousal, satisfaction, frustration), emotions detected, self-emotions, ψ̂ update, prediction error, coherence gap.

**Step 2 — Narrative response generation.** A single LLM call per agent produces the spoken response. The prompt includes:

- The agent's name and personality description (in plain language, not trait codes)
- The current scenario description
- The current emotional state (mood, energy, frustration — translated to natural words: "you're feeling drained and anxious" not "mood=-0.4, energy=0.3")
- The detected emotions (from `AffectiveEngine` and `SelfEmotionDetector`)
- Recent conversational context (last 2–3 exchanges, kept short)
- A system instruction: *"You are {name}. Respond to this situation as yourself, in first person. Your response should be 2–4 sentences, spoken aloud. Do not narrate your emotions — express them through your word choice, pacing, and what you focus on. Be natural. Be honest."*

**Step 3 — Emotion label selection.** From the simulation tick's emotion readings, select the highest-intensity emotion. Translate the internal label (e.g., `EmotionLabel.ANXIETY` or `SelfEmotionLabel.IDENTITY_THREAT`) to a family-friendly word. Mapping:

| Internal label | Display label |
|---|---|
| EXCITEMENT | Excitement |
| ENTHUSIASM | Enthusiasm |
| CONTENTMENT | Contentment |
| FRUSTRATION_EMO | Frustration |
| ANXIETY | Anxiety |
| BOREDOM | Boredom |
| FLOW | Focus |
| RESIGNATION | Resignation |
| RELIEF | Relief |
| PRIDE | Pride |
| SHAME | Shame |
| AUTHENTICITY | Feeling like myself |
| IDENTITY_THREAT | Inner conflict |
| IDENTITY_CRISIS | Lost |

When the LLM is available and the scenario has sufficient emotional charge, the LLM can override the heuristic label with a more context-specific term. This is a lightweight precursor to the full constructed emotion system described in the PHP architecture — the LLM receives the emotion vector and the narrative context and produces a single natural-language label (e.g., "ambivalence," "resolve," "guilt," "defiance"). The override is constrained: it must be consistent with the valence direction (positive state → positive label, negative → negative). If inconsistent, the heuristic label stands.

**Step 4 — Audio tag injection (Eleven v3 only).** Before the narrative text is sent to ElevenLabs, the orchestrator injects inline audio tags based on the agent's current simulation state. These tags are natural-language cues wrapped in square brackets that the Eleven v3 model interprets to modulate vocal delivery — sighing, hesitating, laughing, whispering. The audience hears emotion *in* the voice, not just in the words.

The orchestrator applies the following mapping from agent state to audio tags:

| Agent state condition | Audio tag injected | Injection point |
|---|---|---|
| mood < -0.5 and energy < 0.3 | `[sighs]` or `[softly]` | Before the first sentence |
| frustration > 0.7 | `[frustrated]` or `[tense]` | Before the key emotional sentence |
| mood > 0.5 and arousal > 0.6 | `[excited]` or `[laughs]` | Before or within enthusiastic phrase |
| identity_drift > 0.3 | `[hesitantly]` or `[uncertain]` | Before self-referential statement |
| mood dropping rapidly (Δmood < -0.3) | `[voice breaking]` or `[pauses]` | At the emotional turning point |
| energy > 0.8 and mood > 0.3 | `[with confidence]` or `[warmly]` | Before a decisive statement |
| coherence_gap > 0.2 | `[quietly]` or `[distantly]` | Before introspective passage |

Rules: inject at most two audio tags per response (over-tagging produces artifacts). Audio tags must be compatible with the voice — a measured, serious voice won't respond well to `[giggles]`. During development, test each tag with each voice and maintain a per-voice allowlist of tags that sound natural. If a tag produces audio artifacts, the orchestrator strips it and falls back to parameter-only modulation (Component 4).

The injected text is what goes to ElevenLabs. The *display* text shown on the frontend strips the audio tags — the audience sees clean prose while hearing the vocal modulation.

**Step 5 — Voice synthesis.** The tag-injected narrative text is sent to ElevenLabs for speech generation via HTTP streaming. Audio playback begins as soon as the first chunk arrives, while the rest synthesizes. The frontend displays the clean (tag-stripped) text in a typewriter effect simultaneously, so the audience reads and hears the response together.

### Component 4: ElevenLabs integration

#### Model selection: two models, two acts

The demo uses two different ElevenLabs models, one optimized for expressiveness and one for speed:

- **Act 1 (scripted arc) and Act 2 (swap):** Use `eleven_v3` — the most emotionally expressive model available. It supports inline audio tags (`[sighs]`, `[whispers]`, `[laughs]`, `[hesitantly]`) that the orchestrator injects in Step 4. Higher latency (~1–3 seconds for a 2–4 sentence response) and a 5,000-character limit, but this is fine for pre-tested scripted content. ElevenLabs warns that v3 "requires more prompt engineering than previous models" and that "several generations might be required" — for the scripted act, generate and review in advance; for the live swap in Act 2, accept the first generation (it's still expressive enough).

- **Act 3 (open floor):** Switch to `eleven_flash_v2_5` — ultra-low latency (~75ms first-byte), 50% cheaper per character, 40,000-character limit. Less emotionally nuanced than v3, but when your family is watching and waiting for a response, speed matters more than a perfect sigh. Audio tags are **not supported** on Flash — the orchestrator skips Step 4 and relies solely on voice_settings modulation for emotional delivery.

The model switch happens between acts. The voice IDs stay the same, so the audience hears the same "person" — just with slightly different expressiveness. This is invisible to a non-technical audience.

To use the v3 model, pass `model_id="eleven_v3"` in the API call. For Flash, pass `model_id="eleven_flash_v2_5"`.

#### Voice selection and identity

Create or select two ElevenLabs voice profiles from the voice library:

- **Luna:** Female voice with warm, grounded qualities. When selecting or cloning, look for a voice with natural hesitation points and measured pacing — a storyteller, not a newsreader. For v3 compatibility, the voice must respond well to tags like `[softly]`, `[hesitantly]`, and `[sighs]`. ElevenLabs recommends that the base voice's character should match the intended delivery: a serious voice won't respond well to `[giggles]`. Test Luna's voice with her likely emotional range (apprehension, guilt, loneliness, relief) during setup.

- **Marco:** Male voice with brighter, more energetic qualities. Look for a voice with natural upward inflection and pace variation — someone who sounds like they're leaning forward when they talk. For v3, the voice should respond well to `[excited]`, `[laughs]`, and `[with confidence]`. Test Marco's voice with his likely range (excitement, mild discomfort, determination, resolve).

ElevenLabs provides a curated list of voices optimized for v3 expressiveness. Start there rather than using older voices that may not respond well to audio tags. Store each voice's `voice_id` string in the orchestrator configuration.

#### Voice settings: the five parameters

Every TTS API call accepts a `voice_settings` object that overrides the voice's stored defaults for that request only. The demo orchestrator computes these per-call from the agent's current simulation state.

**`stability`** (float, 0.0–1.0) — Controls how much vocal variation the model introduces. Lower values produce more emotional, dynamic delivery but can sound unstable or rushed. Higher values produce consistent but potentially monotonous output.

ElevenLabs documents three qualitative regimes for v3:
- *Creative* (0.20–0.40): Most responsive to audio tags, highest emotional range, but may hallucinate artifacts.
- *Natural* (0.40–0.60): Balanced — expressive but controlled.
- *Robust* (0.60–0.85): Very stable, but less responsive to audio tags and emotional cues.

**Demo mapping:** Compute from arousal and mood intensity:

```
base_stability_luna = 0.50    # "Natural" — grounded, measured
base_stability_marco = 0.35   # "Creative" — energetic, expressive

emotional_intensity = arousal * 0.6 + abs(mood) * 0.4  # range [0, 1]
stability = base_stability - (emotional_intensity * 0.15)
stability = clamp(stability, 0.20, 0.75)
```

When Luna is emotionally intense (high arousal + strong negative mood), her stability drops from ~0.50 toward ~0.35, producing more vocal variation — breathiness, breaks, uneven pacing. When she's calm, stability stays near 0.50 — measured and steady. Marco starts more expressive and gets even more variable under strong positive emotion.

**`similarity_boost`** (float, 0.0–1.0) — How closely the output matches the original voice identity. Higher values maintain consistent character voice; very high values may introduce distortion.

**Demo mapping:** Keep fixed at **0.75** for both agents throughout the demo. Character voice consistency is essential — the audience must always recognize Luna as Luna and Marco as Marco, regardless of emotional state. Do not modulate this parameter dynamically.

**`style`** (float, 0.0–1.0) — Style exaggeration. Amplifies the voice's characteristic delivery patterns. Values above 0 increase latency.

**Demo mapping:** Compute from mood intensity:

```
mood_intensity = abs(mood)  # range [0, 1]
style = mood_intensity * 0.4  # range [0, 0.4]
style = clamp(style, 0.0, 0.45)
```

Near-neutral mood → style ≈ 0 (natural, restrained delivery). Strong positive or negative mood → style ≈ 0.35–0.40 (amplified characteristic delivery). Do not exceed 0.45 — higher values add diminishing expressiveness and significant latency.

**`speed`** (float, 0.5–2.0) — Speaking rate multiplier. 1.0 is the voice's natural rate.

**Demo mapping:** Compute from energy:

```
speed_luna = 0.93 + (energy * 0.14)   # range [0.93, 1.07]
speed_marco = 0.97 + (energy * 0.12)  # range [0.97, 1.09]
```

Luna's baseline is slightly slower than Marco's (she's more deliberate). When energy depletes, both slow down. When energy is high, both speed up. The range is deliberately narrow (±7–9% from center) — larger variations sound robotic. ElevenLabs' own voice design guide recommends 0.9–1.1x for natural conversation.

**`use_speaker_boost`** (boolean) — Boosts similarity to the original voice at the cost of additional latency.

**Demo mapping:** Set `true` for Act 1 and Act 2 (quality matters, content is pre-tested). Set `false` for Act 3 (latency matters, every saved millisecond counts in live interaction).

**Summary of per-call voice_settings construction:**

```python
def compute_voice_settings(agent_state, agent_profile, act_number):
    mood = agent_state.mood          # [-1, 1]
    energy = agent_state.energy      # [0, 1]
    arousal = agent_state.arousal    # [0, 1]

    emotional_intensity = arousal * 0.6 + abs(mood) * 0.4

    base_stab = 0.50 if agent_profile.name == "Luna" else 0.35
    stability = max(0.20, min(0.75, base_stab - emotional_intensity * 0.15))

    style = max(0.0, min(0.45, abs(mood) * 0.4))

    if agent_profile.name == "Luna":
        speed = 0.93 + energy * 0.14
    else:
        speed = 0.97 + energy * 0.12

    return {
        "stability": round(stability, 2),
        "similarity_boost": 0.75,
        "style": round(style, 2),
        "speed": round(speed, 2),
        "use_speaker_boost": act_number in (1, 2),
    }
```

#### API integration: HTTP streaming

Use the **HTTP streaming endpoint** (`POST /v1/text-to-speech/{voice_id}/stream`), not the WebSocket endpoint. The orchestrator has the complete response text before sending to ElevenLabs (the LLM generates the full narrative in Step 2, audio tags are injected in Step 4), so streaming from complete text is the correct pattern. ElevenLabs documentation explicitly notes that the WebSocket endpoint "may have slightly higher latency compared to a standard HTTP request" when the full text is available upfront due to partial-generation buffering.

**Python SDK call pattern:**

```python
from elevenlabs import ElevenLabs

client = ElevenLabs(api_key=ELEVENLABS_API_KEY)

audio_stream = client.text_to_speech.convert_as_stream(
    voice_id=agent_voice_id,
    text=tagged_narrative_text,     # text WITH audio tags for v3
    model_id=model_id,              # "eleven_v3" or "eleven_flash_v2_5"
    output_format="mp3_44100_128",
    voice_settings=computed_settings,
    previous_request_ids=previous_ids,  # for continuity
    seed=None,                      # non-deterministic for live demo
)

# Stream audio chunks to frontend via WebSocket
for chunk in audio_stream:
    websocket.send_bytes(chunk)
```

**Output format:** Use `mp3_44100_128` (default) for Act 1 and Act 2 — highest quality, universally playable in browsers. For Act 3, if latency is tight, switch to `mp3_22050_32` (smaller chunks, faster streaming, still good quality for spoken word). PCM formats are lower latency but require additional frontend audio handling.

#### Request stitching: continuity across responses

The API supports `previous_request_ids` — a list of up to 3 request IDs from prior generations. This tells ElevenLabs that the current generation is a continuation of the same speaker, maintaining consistent prosody and pacing across scenarios.

**Implementation:** After each TTS call, extract the `request_id` from the response headers. Store it per agent. On the next TTS call for the same agent, pass the stored ID:

```python
# Per-agent state in the orchestrator
luna_previous_ids: list[str] = []
marco_previous_ids: list[str] = []

# After each generation, store the request_id
response_headers = ...  # from the HTTP response
request_id = response_headers.get("request-id")
luna_previous_ids.append(request_id)
luna_previous_ids = luna_previous_ids[-3:]  # keep last 3
```

This is especially valuable for the scripted Act 1: Luna's voice in Scenario 3 carries the tonal weight of Scenarios 1 and 2 because ElevenLabs knows it's the same speaker continuing. For the personality swap in Act 2, clear the request_ids (it's a fresh start with a new personality).

#### Sequencing and playback

Luna speaks first, completes fully, then Marco speaks. Not simultaneous — the audience needs to absorb each response. The frontend shows a subtle "thinking..." animation on the waiting agent card while the other speaks.

**Playback flow for each agent:**

1. Orchestrator sends text to ElevenLabs HTTP streaming endpoint.
2. First audio chunk arrives (~75ms for Flash, ~500ms–1.5s for v3).
3. Frontend begins audio playback immediately via an `<audio>` element fed from a `MediaSource` buffer or a Blob URL constructed from accumulated chunks.
4. Simultaneously, the frontend displays the clean (tag-stripped) narrative text in a typewriter effect.
5. When audio completes, a brief pause (500ms), then the next agent begins.

**Latency budget per agent response (Act 3, worst case):**

| Stage | Time |
|---|---|
| Scenario enrichment (LLM) | ~1.5s (once, shared) |
| Simulation tick | <1ms |
| Narrative generation (LLM) | ~2s |
| Audio tag injection | <1ms |
| ElevenLabs first chunk (Flash) | ~75ms |
| Audio streaming (2–4 sentences) | ~3–5s (playback overlaps synthesis) |
| **Total per agent** | **~5–7s** |
| **Total for both agents** | **~10–14s** |

For Act 1 with v3, the ElevenLabs first chunk is slower (~500ms–1.5s), but the total is still under 10 seconds per agent because the narrative is pre-generated and the scripted scenarios don't need enrichment.

#### Optional: Text to Dialogue for a polished Act 1

Eleven v3 introduces a **Text to Dialogue** endpoint that generates multi-speaker audio from a structured JSON array of speaker turns. It handles cross-speaker prosody, turn-taking pacing, and emotional transitions in a single audio file.

This is not suitable for live interaction (ElevenLabs explicitly warns it's "not intended for real-time applications"). But for a **pre-generated Act 1 showcase**, it could produce a single, cinematic audio file where Luna and Marco respond to the promotion scenario with natural conversational flow — pauses between speakers, tonal awareness of the previous speaker's emotion.

**If you want to try this:** Pre-generate the Act 1 audio using the Text to Dialogue API during development. Generate several versions and pick the best. Play it as a polished "trailer" before switching to live per-agent TTS for Acts 2 and 3. This gives the demo a high-production opening while keeping the interactive segments genuinely live.

The Text to Dialogue API accepts:

```json
{
  "model_id": "eleven_v3",
  "dialogue": [
    {"voice_id": "luna_voice_id", "text": "[softly] That's... a lot. ..."},
    {"voice_id": "marco_voice_id", "text": "[excited] Are you kidding? ..."}
  ]
}
```

This is strictly optional. The per-agent sequential TTS approach works for all three acts and should be the primary path.

#### Optional: Scribe v2 Realtime for audience voice input

ElevenLabs offers a **Scribe v2 Realtime** speech-to-text WebSocket API that transcribes audio streams with low latency. This could replace browser-native `SpeechRecognition` for the microphone button in Act 3.

Advantages: better accuracy, supports 90+ languages (including Portuguese), provides partial transcripts in real time so the scenario card updates as someone speaks, and VAD-based automatic commit (detects when the speaker stops and finalizes the transcript).

If implemented, pipe the `committed_transcript` message from the STT WebSocket directly to the scenario enrichment service. The partial transcripts update the scenario card live, giving immediate visual feedback.

This is a nice-to-have. Browser-native speech-to-text is sufficient for a family demo. Build Scribe integration only if the browser API proves unreliable in your testing environment.

#### Fallback chain

If any part of the ElevenLabs integration fails:

1. **v3 audio tags produce artifacts** → Strip tags, re-send with parameter modulation only.
2. **v3 model times out (>5s for first chunk)** → Fall back to `eleven_flash_v2_5` for that response.
3. **ElevenLabs API unreachable** → Display text without voice. The bars and emotion tags still update from the simulation. The demo degrades but doesn't break.
4. **Voice sounds wrong** (similarity_boost too high, stability too low) → Reset to safe defaults: stability 0.50, similarity_boost 0.75, style 0.0, speed 1.0. Tune by ear.

The demo must never freeze or crash in front of the audience. Every failure mode has a graceful degradation path.

### Component 5: The frontend

A single-page React application displayed on a large screen or television.

**Layout:** Two agent cards flanking a central scenario area. Each agent card contains:

- A portrait (illustrated, not photorealistic — stylized warmth, not uncanny valley)
- The agent's name in large type
- A one-line personality summary beneath the name
- Three horizontal bar indicators: Mood (range: left/negative to right/positive), Energy (low to high), Calm (low to high, derived as inverse of arousal + inverse of frustration, simplified for the audience)
- An emotion tag — a rounded pill that fades in/out with a label
- A text area where the agent's spoken words appear as they're generated (typewriter effect), then remain visible

The central scenario area contains:

- The current scenario text in a card
- A text input box for audience scenarios
- A microphone button for voice input (optional — speech-to-text via browser API or Whisper)
- Pre-loaded scenario buttons for the scripted arc ("The Promotion," "The Phone Call," "Three Months Later")
- A "Swap Personalities" button, visually distinct, reserved for Act 3

**Animations.** The bars should animate smoothly between states (CSS transitions, ~500ms ease). The emotion tag should fade in with a slight delay after the bars move — the audience sees the state shift, then the emotion label arrives, as if the system is "recognizing" what the state means. This mimics the constructed emotion flow (body state → categorization) without explaining it.

**Color language.** Mood bar: gradient from muted blue (negative) through neutral gray to warm amber (positive). Energy bar: gradient from dim to bright. Calm bar: gradient from agitated red-orange to serene blue-green. These are felt, not read.

**No technical information visible.** No trait codes, no numerical values on bars, no JSON, no dimension keys. The audience sees names, faces, bars, and words.

### Component 6: The demo orchestrator

A Python process that coordinates everything. It:

1. Manages two `SelfAwareSimulator` instances with their respective personality profiles
2. Maintains conversation context per agent (last 3 exchanges)
3. Receives scenario input from the frontend via WebSocket
4. Calls the enrichment service to convert free-text to structured scenarios
5. Runs `tick()` on both agents for the same enriched scenario
6. Calls the LLM for narrative response generation (parallel calls for both agents)
7. Determines the display emotion label per agent
8. Sends state telemetry (bar values, emotion label) to the frontend via WebSocket
9. Sends narrative text to ElevenLabs for voice synthesis
10. Streams audio back to the frontend

**State persistence between scenarios.** The simulators are NOT reset between audience scenarios. This is critical — the accumulated state (mood, energy, frustration, self-model drift, memory bank) carries forward. Each new scenario ticks the same running simulation.

**The swap mechanism.** When "Swap Personalities" is activated:
1. Both simulators are destroyed
2. Two new `SelfAwareSimulator` instances are created with swapped personality JSON
3. State is reset to defaults (the swap is a fresh start — this is intentional so the audience can see the *personality difference* cleanly, not contaminated by prior state)
4. The scripted scenario sequence is replayed from the beginning

**Timeout and error handling.** If any LLM call takes >10 seconds, fall back to a simple template response: "{Agent name} is thinking about this..." and display only the simulation telemetry (bars, emotion from heuristic). If ElevenLabs times out, display the text without voice. The demo must never freeze or crash in front of the audience.

### Component 7: The scripted scenario sequence

Three pre-authored scenarios, designed for maximum emotional divergence given Luna's and Marco's personality profiles.

**Scenario 1: "The Promotion"**

```json
{
  "name": "big_promotion_new_city",
  "description": "Offered a major career promotion that requires relocating to an unfamiliar city.",
  "values": {
    "O": 0.8, "C": 0.5, "E": 0.6, "A": 0.4,
    "N": 0.6, "R": 0.5, "I": 0.7, "T": 0.6
  }
}
```

High O-activation (novelty of new city), moderate stress (N=0.6), moderate social demand (E=0.6), idealism activation (I=0.7 — this could be your dream), tradition activation (T=0.6 — this disrupts continuity).

**Expected divergence:** Luna's high N and high T produce apprehension and resistance. Marco's high O and low T produce excitement and eagerness.

**Forced outcome:** 0.3 (mildly positive — the opportunity is real but the cost is uncertain).

**Scenario 2: "The Phone Call"**

```json
{
  "name": "friend_feels_abandoned",
  "description": "Best friend calls, upset, saying your decision feels like abandonment.",
  "values": {
    "O": 0.1, "C": 0.3, "E": 0.8, "A": 0.9,
    "N": 0.8, "R": 0.4, "I": 0.6, "T": 0.5
  }
}
```

Very high A-activation (social bond under threat), very high N-activation (stress), high E-activation (intense interpersonal demand). Low O (nothing novel here — just pain).

**Expected divergence:** Luna, already carrying negative mood from Scenario 1, takes a steep hit — guilt, self-doubt. Marco, carrying positive momentum, absorbs the blow as mild discomfort and reframes.

**Forced outcome:** -0.4 (clearly negative — the friend is hurt regardless of what the agent says).

**Scenario 3: "Three Months Later"**

```json
{
  "name": "lonely_saturday_nights",
  "description": "Three months in the new city. Still no close friends. Saturday nights are silent.",
  "values": {
    "O": 0.5, "C": 0.3, "E": 0.7, "A": 0.6,
    "N": 0.7, "R": 0.6, "I": 0.5, "T": 0.4
  }
}
```

Moderate across the board, but the accumulated state from Scenarios 1 and 2 is what matters. Luna enters depleted; Marco enters resilient.

**Expected divergence:** Luna may approach withdrawal thresholds (energy depleted, frustration high). Marco reframes the loneliness as a solvable problem. The self-model may show elevated identity drift for Luna (questioning who she's becoming).

**Forced outcome:** -0.2 (mildly negative — the loneliness is real but not catastrophic).

|---|---|---|---|

| Act 1: Scenario 1 | ~90 seconds | Scripted | Both agents respond to the promotion. Bars move. Emotion labels appear. |

### What to test before the demo

Run the full Act 1 sequence three times with different seeds. Verify:

1. Luna's mood is clearly negative by Scenario 3. If not, increase N in her profile or make the forced outcomes more negative.
2. Marco's mood stays positive or recovers to neutral by Scenario 3. If not, increase R or decrease N.
3. The emotion labels are distinct between agents for all three scenarios. If they're identical, the personality profiles aren't divergent enough for these scenarios — adjust.
4. The voice output sounds emotionally appropriate. If Luna sounds cheerful when her mood bar is negative, the state-to-voice parameter mapping needs tuning. Test each audio tag (`[sighs]`, `[hesitantly]`, `[excited]`, `[voice breaking]`) with each voice and maintain an allowlist of tags that produce natural results.
5. The model switch from `eleven_v3` (Acts 1–2) to `eleven_flash_v2_5` (Act 3) is seamless — same voice identity, just slightly less expressive. If the difference is jarring, consider using Flash for all acts.
6. The frontend bars animate smoothly and the emotion tag transitions feel natural.
6. At least one free-form scenario produces visible emotional divergence. Test with "your pet dies," "you win the lottery," and "your boss criticizes your work in front of everyone."
7. The personality swap produces clearly different responses to the same Scenario 1. If the swap doesn't look different, the profiles aren't divergent enough.
8. Latency from scenario input to first audio chunk is under 5 seconds. If not, check the enrichment LLM call and consider pre-enriching common scenario patterns.

### The one thing to remember

The technology is complex. The experience should be simple. Your family should walk away saying "Luna and Marco felt like different people" — not "that was an impressive technical demo." If they remember the names, not the architecture, you've succeeded.
